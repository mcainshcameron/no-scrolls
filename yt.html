December 29, 2025

Dwarkesh Patel (with Sarah Paine)

Soviet political economyGorbachev reforms (perestroika/glasnost)Institutions: prices, property rights, legal systems
The discussion argues that Gorbachev’s late-Soviet reforms worsened stagnation because political liberalization preceded (and undermined) the hard institutional prerequisites for economic repair. By devolving authority to quasi-independent enterprises while rejecting core market mechanisms—credible prices, private property, and enforceable contracts—the system lost coherent coordination without gaining functional markets. In that vacuum, allocation shifted from rules to relationships, accelerating corruption and backchannel bargaining. A central claim is that even if reformers wanted a faster transition, the absence of a mature legal system made rapid marketization and decentralization structurally unstable.
Key Insights
  • A major failure mode identified is sequencing: Gorbachev "gave away political power" before stabilizing or fixing underlying economic dysfunction.
  • Decentralizing control without accepting market principles (real prices and property rights) prevents firms from coordinating allocation of scarce resources through transparent signals.
  • When price signals and property rights are viewed as illegitimate, transactions tend to move into informal channels—corruption and backroom deals become the de facto coordination mechanism.
  • Institutional capacity is presented as a hard constraint: effective market reforms require a legal system (contract enforcement, dispute resolution), which cannot be created on a compressed timeline.
  • China is contrasted as drawing the opposite lesson on sequencing: preserve political control while selectively adjusting economic policy, rather than pairing decentralization with weakened central authority.
