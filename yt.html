Dwarkesh Patel (with Sarah Paine)

Soviet political economyCold War geopolitics in AfricaCommodity and oil price shocks
Sarah Paine frames the Soviet Union’s late–Cold War overextension as a financial and political trap accelerated by commodity and oil price collapses. As newly independent states in Africa and elsewhere sought alternatives to Western European influence, the USSR backed a growing roster of partner regimes—many of which depended on commodity exports whose revenues cratered during the late-1970s/1980s downturn. At the same time, the Soviet budget was heavily exposed to oil, so falling oil prices squeezed Moscow’s ability to subsidize allies just as demands rose. The compounding effect was a multi-front crisis: expensive external commitments coincided with synchronized internal ethnic unrest, violating the basic “no two-front wars” logic of maintaining a continental empire.
Key Insights
  • Many Soviet-aligned partners (e.g., Angola, South Yemen, Ethiopia, Nicaragua) were commodity exporters; the late-1970s/1980s recession depressed commodity prices and slashed their export earnings, increasing their dependence on Soviet support.
  • The USSR’s own fiscal position was highly oil-dependent; oil accounted for up to ~55% of the Soviet budget, so the oil price collapse directly reduced its capacity to fund external commitments.
  • By the late 1980s, Moscow faced a “bench of non-performing pals”—client states requiring aid at precisely the moment Soviet revenues weakened.
  • Internal unrest accelerated after Gorbachev’s accession, with student movements and widening ethnic rebellions across distant regions (e.g., Kazakhstan and Yakutia).
  • By around 1990, the scale of internal upheaval is characterized as dozens of simultaneous rebellions (roughly 60–76), creating an unmanageable, multi-front stability challenge.

Matthew Berman

Computer visionVideo segmentationOpen-source AI models (Meta SAM 3)
Matthew Berman showcases Meta’s SAM 3 (Segment Anything Model) as an open-weights, open-source system that performs prompt-driven object segmentation in video. The key value proposition is natural language interaction—users can type a label like “bicycle” or “taxi,” and the model searches across the entire clip to identify and segment matching objects over time, including items that enter the frame mid-video. The demonstration emphasizes practical usability via an interface that returns per-object masks with distinct colors and thumbnails, enabling quick selection and cleanup. Overall, the video positions SAM 3 as a major step toward accessible, high-quality video understanding and editing workflows without proprietary lock-in.
Key Insights
  • SAM 3 supports text-prompted segmentation in video (e.g., typing “bicycle” to find and segment bicycles throughout a clip).
  • The system can discover objects that appear later in the timeline, not just those visible in the first frame, enabling whole-video search-and-segment.
  • Results are organized as independently segmented instances, presented with per-object thumbnails, labels, and color-coded masks for easy review.
  • The workflow includes simple post-processing controls (e.g., deleting unwanted segments/instances) to refine outputs quickly.
  • Meta releases SAM 3 as free/open-source with open weights, lowering barriers for developers and tooling ecosystems to adopt and extend it.
